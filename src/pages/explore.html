Let's Explore

The holy mountain has been popularly known as,

"Sri Pada" since asncient times. "Sri Pada" is also known as "Samanala Kanda" (Butterfly Mountain) due to the swarms of butterflies that surround the sides of the mountain at the beginning of the season. According to ancient beliefs, God Suman Saman is the Protector of Sri Pada; thus, it is also known as "Samanta-Kuta." "Adams Peak," "Rathnagiri," and "Swargarohanam" are a few names used for Sri Pada.

The pilgrimage season traditionally starts on the full moon in December and ends on the full moon in May. The season and off-season are mainly determined by the rain. The season starts in the middle of the North Eastern Monsoon, where this region receives significantly low rainfall, and ends in May, the month in which the South Eastern Monsoon starts. During the off-season months, Peak Wilderness receives a heavy rainfall, and it was difficult and risky to attempt the hike during the off-season in the past, but with recent developments and new technology available, hikers frequently complete off-season hikes now.

There are many traditional customs bound to the climbing of Sri Pada Mountain. The first-time climber of Sri Pada is known as "Kodukarayo". There are three types of Kodukarayo. Child first-timers are called "Kirikodu" while the youngsters are called "Nambarakodu" and the adults are called "Dandukodu". It is customary for devotees to bathe at the stream called "Seetha Gangula" and dress in white clothes before starting the journey.

"Indikatupana" is the place where devotees stop and hang a threaded needle from a shrub. It is said that when the Buddha came to Sri Lanka, he sewed his robe there. People are still collecting needles and threads and dumping them on tree branches, bushes, and everywhere else in the area to celebrate that action. Pilgrims can see the threads as if they were a large spider net. The rock from which the Buddha swung his robe became known as "gettampana”

Devotees usually sing various folk songs while climbing the mountain. At the summit, they ring the bell according to the number of visits to Sri Pada. The main goal of the Sri Pada devotees is to reach the peak of the mountain before the sun rises. This gory view of the sun rising is called "Ira sewaya," which makes for an unforgettable experience.

The words used during the Sri Pada pilgrimage are owed a special place in our culture. 'Nadaya', 'Nade Gura', 'Kodu', 'Kirikodu', 'Hime', 'Karunavai', 'Peweema', 'Kata varadda ganeema', 'Kode', 'Ambalama', and 'Padhmaya' are among them. Sri Pada devotees who climb up and climb down wish each other to have the blessing of God Sumana.

"Lihinihela" is a popular place on the way to Adams Peak. It is a huge precipice, like the world’s end. Behind the valley, a large mountain can be seen. There, the pilgrims yell "Lihini Akke." The echo of their yelling can then be heard by them. There is a sorrowful folk story behind this shouting. In Sinhalese "hela" means the precipice. "Lihinihela" is the home of a woman named "Lihini."

In accordance with folklore, Lihinihela didn't have a valley in the very distant past. There was a small hut, and there lived a mother and her baby in it. One day it began to rain. So Mother came out of the hut to collect clothes. At that time, the earth went down and made this valley. Hut vanished. "Lihini Akka," the mother and her baby, were berried alive. People believe that the spirit of "Lihini Akka" is still in the valley, and this spirit is looking for her baby. So people say that the echo is the voice of "Lihini Akka."
